01

Problem Truth Lens

A diagnostic framework for moving from the visible request to the actual product opportunity worth solving.

1

Decode the ask

Capture what the client, user, or stakeholder is requesting without accepting it as the final solution.

2

Mine signals

Map user pain, workflow gaps, assumptions, data, constraints, and market patterns.

3

Find the root

Identify whether the real issue is visibility, trust, decision delay, cognitive load, or flow breakdown.

4

Frame the bet

Turn the problem into a product thesis with evidence, expected outcome, and next capability path.

Proof chip: reframed request into root problem Metric lens: task time / error rate / adoption Output: opportunity thesis before UI
Key question

Is this the real problem, or only the visible symptom the user/client can describe?

Senior shift

From "make the requested screen" to "define the product move that solves the underlying workflow gap."

Decision evidence

I look for customer pain, business value, workflow frequency, risk, feasibility, and future extensibility before choosing a direction.

What to show in case studies

Before/after problem framing, rejected solution paths, why the chosen direction was more durable, and which metric it was meant to move.

Portfolio copy: I do not start by designing the requested screen. I first diagnose whether the request is the real problem, a symptom, or only one possible solution. This helps me turn vague requirements into product opportunities with clearer user value, business impact, and long-term scalability.

Decision rule

Move forward only when the concept has strong user need, visible business value, realistic build feasibility, and a foundation that can support the next capability without redesigning from zero.

GoBuild / prototype

High value, clear pain, feasible path, scalable base.

IterateRefine direction

Good opportunity, but weak proof, unclear flow, or edge-case risk.

ParkDe-prioritize

Low value, weak signal, heavy effort, or short solution lifetime.

Portfolio copy: I make product design decisions through a decision mesh: user need, business value, feasibility, stakeholder fit, market timing, and solution lifetime. The goal is not to ship the easiest UI, but to choose the product direction with the best long-term return.
